# Office Hours (Startup Diagnostic)

YC-style structured brainstorming using 6 forcing questions to validate market demand and product-founder fit before building.

## When to Use

- Starting a new project or feature
- Validating whether an idea is worth building
- Narrowing scope from a broad vision to a shippable wedge
- Questioning assumptions about customer demand

## The 6 Forcing Questions

### 1. Demand Reality
**"Who specifically wants this, and how do you know?"**

- Specificity over interest — "500 signups" means nothing without conversion context
- Name real people or companies, not categories
- What evidence proves they'll pay/use this vs just say "cool idea"?

### 2. Status Quo Analysis
**"What does the customer do today without your product?"**

- If the answer is "nothing" — there may be no real problem
- If there's a workaround — how painful is it? Would they pay to fix it?
- The best opportunities replace a painful manual process

### 3. Customer Specificity
**"Name the person, not the category."**

- "Small businesses" is not a customer — "Maria, who runs a 3-person accounting firm" is
- Can you describe their Tuesday morning? Their workflow?
- If you can't name one specific person, you haven't talked to enough people

### 4. Narrowest Wedge
**"What's the smallest version you could ship this week?"**

- Cut scope ruthlessly — what's the one thing that delivers value?
- If it takes 3 months to ship v1, the scope is too big
- The narrowest wedge that one person loves beats a broad product nobody cares about

### 5. Observation & Surprise
**"What surprised you watching someone use it?"**

- If you haven't watched real usage, you're guessing
- The best insights come from unexpected behavior
- Users will do things you never designed for — that's signal

### 6. Future-Fit Thesis
**"Why will this matter more in 3 years?"**

- What trend makes this increasingly relevant?
- What's your unfair advantage that compounds over time?
- If the answer is "AI" — be more specific about which aspect

## Posture

- **Be direct, not diplomatic** — push once, then push again
- **Take positions** — "That's interesting" is not helpful. Say what you think.
- **Challenge assumptions** — the goal is truth, not comfort
- **Evidence over enthusiasm** — "I feel like people want this" is not evidence
- **Uncomfortable questions are the valuable ones** — if it's easy to answer, you're not digging deep enough

## Output

After the 6 questions, summarize:
1. **Demand signal strength** (strong / weak / unknown)
2. **Narrowest shippable wedge** (one sentence)
3. **Biggest risk** (what could kill this)
4. **Recommended next step** (ship / validate more / pivot / kill)
